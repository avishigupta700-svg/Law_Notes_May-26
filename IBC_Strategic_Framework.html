FRAMEWORK 01

The Insolvency–Bankruptcy–Liquidation Continuum

Understanding how a payment default travels from insolvency to resolution or dissolution

Decision-Tree Logic: The Debtor's Journey

IF-THEN-ELSE · INSOLVENCY PATHWAY
IF

A debtor (individual or corporate) fails to pay debt when due → debtor is insolvent. Insolvency = a financial state (not a legal status).

THEN

Creditor or debtor triggers the IBC resolution mechanism → CIRP commences (for corporates) or Insolvency Resolution Process (for individuals/firms).

IF

Resolution succeeds → Resolution Plan approved by CoC + NCLT. Corporate debtor revived as going concern. Moratorium ceases.

ELSE

Resolution fails → Liquidation (for corporates) or Bankruptcy (for non-corporates). Assets sold; proceeds distributed per §53 priority.

NOTE

Insolvency ≠ Bankruptcy. Insolvency is the state; bankruptcy is the legal conclusion. Not all insolvencies lead to bankruptcy/liquidation.

Cause 1 — Financial Failure

Persistent mismatch between payments and receivables — business model generates revenue but cash-flow timing breaks down. Short-term liquidity crisis.

Cause 2 — Business Failure

Business model itself collapses; revenues insufficient to meet obligations. Also includes avoidance transactions that trigger insolvency artificially.

Applicability Framework — §2

WHO DOES IBC APPLY TO?

Companies

Under Companies Act 2013 or any previous law; also companies under special acts (to the extent not inconsistent)

LLPs

Under LLP Act 2008

Notified Entities

Any body incorporated under any law as notified by Central Government

Personal Guarantors

To corporate debtors — treated as separate class; applicable w.e.f. 23-11-2017 (NCLT jurisdiction)

Partnership & Proprietorship Firms

Part III; DRT is AA

Individuals

Other than personal guarantors; Part III; DRT is AA

NON-APPLICABILITY — CRITICAL EXCEPTION

IBC does not apply to Financial Service Providers (§3(7)) — banks, insurance companies, mutual funds, etc. However, §227 allows Central Government to notify FSPs (e.g., NBFCs with asset size ≥ ₹500 crore notified on 18-11-2019 for IBC proceedings).

§238 — The "Nuclear Clause"

IBC overrides all other laws, including GST, Income Tax, SARFAESI, Companies Act. Distribution per §53 (liquidation priority) prevails over any other statutory first-charge on assets.

The CIRP Initiation & Admission Framework

Triggering the process — who files, what documents, what timeline

Default Threshold — §4

Original: ₹1 lakh
Current: ₹1 crore
(Notified 24-03-2020; COVID amendment)

Default Definition — §3(12)

Non-payment when whole or any part of the debt amount is due & payable and not paid — including instalments

COVID Suspension — §10A

No CIRP for defaults arising on or after 25-03-2020 (extended twice; applicable up to 25-03-2021)

Who Can Initiate CIRP? — §6

Three-Stage Application Process (SC in Surendra Trading Co.)

Stage 1Registry scrutinizes completeness; 7-day notice to cure defects (directory, not mandatory)
→
Stage 2AA admits or rejects within 14 days (directory); 7-day cure notice before rejection
→
Stage 3If admitted → CIRP commences from date of admission (Insolvency Commencement Date)

Persons NOT Entitled to Initiate CIRP — §11

  • Corporate debtor already undergoing CIRP or PPIRP
  • Corporate debtor that completed CIRP within past 12 months
  • Corporate debtor where resolution plan approved under PPIRP in past 12 months
  • Corporate debtor (or FC) that violated terms of resolution plan approved within last 12 months
  • Corporate debtor under a liquidation order
  • FC or OC of a corporate debtor undergoing PPIRP

Exception: §11 Explanation 2 — does not prevent a CD from initiating CIRP against another corporate debtor.

Withdrawal Framework — §12A + Regulation 30A

IF-THEN · WITHDRAWAL SCENARIOS
IF

Before CoC constitution → applicant files through IRP → AA approval (no CoC vote required)

IF

After CoC constitution but before EoI → CoC considers within 7 days → 90% voting share approval → RP submits to AA within 3 days → AA passes withdrawal order

IF

After EoI issued → Same procedure as above but applicant must state reasons for withdrawal post-EoI

NOTE

SC in Ashok Rajani v. Beacon Trusteeship: Before CoC constitution, no bar on withdrawal by applicant of §7 application already admitted. CoC vote threshold only applies once CoC is formed.

FRAMEWORK 04

The Moratorium & Public Announcement Framework

"Calm period" — freezing actions against the corporate debtor

ContextTrigger PointLegal RequirementStrategic Impact
Moratorium Declaration Insolvency commencement date (§14) AA passes order on the same day as admission All suits, executions, asset transfers, security enforcement frozen — creates breathing room for resolution
Duration From commencement date Until CIRP completion (max 330 days) Ceases earlier if AA approves resolution plan or passes liquidation order
Essential Supplies Throughout moratorium Cannot be terminated / interrupted (§14(2)) Protects going-concern value; RP can designate "critical" supplies (§14(2A))
Guarantor Exception Moratorium on CD §14(1) does NOT apply to surety/guarantor Creditors can pursue personal guarantors even while moratorium is in force on CD
Licences & Permits During moratorium Cannot be suspended/terminated on grounds of insolvency Subject to condition: no default in current dues for such licence/permit during moratorium

Acts Prohibited Under Moratorium — §14(1)

Prohibition A

Institution or continuation of suits / proceedings against the CD (including execution of decrees)

Prohibition B

Transfer, encumbering, alienating or disposing of CD's assets or any legal/beneficial interest

Prohibition C

Action to foreclose, recover or enforce security interest (including SARFAESI actions)

Prohibition D

Recovery of property by owner/lessor if property is in possession of the CD

Public Announcement — §13 & §15

IRP must make public announcement within 3 days of appointment. Announcement must include:

Name & address of corporate debtor
Authority with which CD is incorporated
Details of IRP (responsible for receiving claims)
Penalties for false / misleading claims
Last date for submission of claims
Date on which CIRP ends

Expenses borne by applicant initially; reimbursed by CoC to the extent it ratifies them.

FRAMEWORK 06

The Resolution Plan Framework

From resolution applicant eligibility to NCLT approval — what must the plan contain?

Persons NOT Eligible as Resolution Applicant — §29A

SECTION 29A — DISQUALIFICATION GROUNDS (KEY FOR IBS CASE STUDIES)

  • Undischarged insolvent
  • Wilful defaulter as per RBI guidelines under Banking Regulation Act 1949
  • Account (or account of CD under their control) classified as NPA for ≥ 1 year — unless all overdue + interest + charges paid before submitting resolution plan
  • Convicted for offence with imprisonment ≥ 2 years (Twelfth Schedule) or ≥ 7 years (any other law) — exception: after 2 years of release from prison
  • Disqualified director under Companies Act 2013
  • SEBI prohibited from trading in securities or accessing markets
  • Promoter/management of CD where preferential / undervalued / extortionate / fraudulent transaction was the subject of an AA order
  • Executed guarantee for a creditor where CIRP has been admitted and the guarantee remains unpaid
  • Subject to similar disability under any foreign jurisdiction
  • Has a connected person ineligible under any of the above (promoter, management, holding/subsidiary of applicant)

Exception: Financial entities not related to CD are exempt from the NPA clause.

Mandatory Contents of a Valid Resolution Plan — §30(2)

Payment Priority

Insolvency resolution process costs paid first — before all other debts

Operational Creditors

Amount ≥ liquidation value (§53) or waterfall value, whichever is higher

Dissenting FCs

Amount ≥ what they would have received in liquidation per §53

Post-Approval Management

Must specify how CD will be managed after approval

Implementation & Supervision

Mechanism for implementation and monitoring

Legal Compliance

Must not contravene any law in force; Competition Act combination approval if applicable

Resolution Plan — Decision Logic

IF-THEN-ELSE · RESOLUTION PLAN OUTCOME
IF

CoC approves plan by 66% vote → RP submits to AA (NCLT)

IF

NCLT satisfied plan meets §30(2) requirements → Approval Order: plan binding on CD, employees, creditors, guarantors, Central/State Governments

THEN

Moratorium ceases; RP forwards CIRP records to IBBI database; resolution applicant obtains necessary approvals within 1 year

ELSE

If NCLT rejects plan (non-compliance of §30(2)) → Liquidation order passed

SC

Essar Steel (2019): NCLT / NCLAT cannot reverse commercial decisions of CoC. Successful resolution applicant starts on a "fresh slate" — all liabilities beyond the plan are extinguished.

Post-Approval Immunity — §32A

If the resolution plan results in change of management/control to a person who was NOT a promoter / management / related party of the CD (and not a conspirator per investigative authority findings), then:

  • ✓ Corporate debtor's liability for pre-CIRP offences ceases
  • ✓ No action against CD's property for pre-CIRP offences covered by the plan
  • ✗ Individual promoters / officers-in-default still liable to prosecution for their personal involvement

Grounds for Appeal Against Approved Resolution Plan — §61(3)

Ground 1

Plan contravenes any law in force

Ground 2

Material irregularity in RP's exercise of powers during CIRP period

Ground 3

Debts of operational creditors not provided for as specified by IBBI Board

Ground 4

CIRP costs not given priority for repayment in the plan

FRAMEWORK 07

The Liquidation Process Framework

When resolution fails — winding up and distributing value

Events Triggering Liquidation — §33

Trigger 1

No resolution plan received before expiry of CIRP period (180 / 330 days)

Trigger 2

AA rejects the resolution plan for non-compliance with §30(2)

Trigger 3

CoC resolves to liquidate by 66% vote — at any time during CIRP before confirmation of resolution plan

Trigger 4

Corporate debtor contravenes approved resolution plan → affected person applies to AA → AA determines contravention → liquidation order

Key Principle: CIRP always precedes liquidation. There is no provision for direct liquidation without undergoing CIRP first under the Code.

Distribution Waterfall — §53 Priority Order

PriorityCreditor/Claim Category
1stInsolvency resolution process costs + liquidation costs (including liquidator's fees)
2ndWorkmen's dues (24 months preceding liquidation) + Secured creditors who relinquish security
3rdEmployee wages & dues (12 months preceding liquidation)
4thFinancial debts to unsecured creditors
5thCentral / State Government dues (including tax — overridden by §238)
6thRemaining debts & dues
7thPreference shareholders
LastEquity shareholders / partners

Consequences of Liquidation Order — §33

Legal Proceedings

No new suits/proceedings against CD; liquidator can file with AA's prior approval (subject to §52 exception for financial sector transactions)

Employees

Order = notice of discharge to officers, employees, workmen — unless business continued as going concern by liquidator

Liquidation Estate

All assets of CD vest in liquidation estate; proceeds distributed per §53 waterfall

Moratorium Effect

Moratorium imposed on pending proceedings; assets (including proceeds) vest in liquidation estate

FRAMEWORK 08

IBS Compliance Indicators — The Non-Negotiables

High-yield points for case study analysis — marks go here

CRITICAL NON-NEGOTIABLES FOR IBS EXAM

  • Threshold: CIRP only triggers if default ≥ ₹1 crore (current threshold from 24-03-2020; original ₹1 lakh is outdated — examiners test this)
  • IBC ≠ Recovery Tool: Per preamble and judicial interpretation (NCLAT in M/s Amsons case), IBC cannot be used merely to recover debts or interest — it is a resolution mechanism
  • §238 Overriding Effect: IBC prevails over GST, Income Tax, SARFAESI in distribution of liquidation proceeds. Tax claims are subordinated to §53 waterfall
  • Moratorium does NOT protect guarantors: Creditors can pursue personal/corporate guarantors even while moratorium is in force against the CD
  • CoC is supreme on commercial decisions: NCLT/NCLAT cannot substitute their view for CoC's commercial wisdom (Essar Steel SC ruling 2019)
  • Resolution Applicant — Fresh Slate: Upon approval of resolution plan, all pre-plan liabilities of CD (beyond the plan) are extinguished. New management takes over a clean entity
  • 330-day outer limit is mandatory: CIRP must complete within 330 days (180 + 90 extension + legal proceedings time). In rare exceptional cases, AA/Appellate Tribunal may extend beyond 330 days
  • §29A ineligibility is absolute: A wilful defaulter or undischarged insolvent CANNOT submit a resolution plan — no waiver possible
  • RP cannot act without CoC approval for §28 actions: Any such unauthorized action is void — zero legal effect
  • IRP is appointed on Day 0: Management control shifts on insolvency commencement date — not after any further process
  • Operational Creditors must send demand notice FIRST: Cannot file §9 application without first issuing demand notice and waiting 10 days
  • Pre-packaged IRP (PPIRP) for MSMEs: A separate fast-track resolution introduced by 2021 Amendment — alternative to CIRP for MSMEs

IRAC 1 — Admissibility of CIRP Application (Financial Creditor)

IRAC · TEMPLATE · §7 APPLICATION BY FINANCIAL CREDITOR
I

ISSUE

Whether [Name/FC] can file an application under §7 of IBC 2016 to initiate CIRP against [corporate debtor] for non-payment of [financial debt] amounting to ₹[X].

R

RULE

Under §7, a financial creditor (alone or jointly) may file before NCLT when a default has occurred (§3(12) — non-payment of whole or part of debt when due). Minimum default threshold: ₹1 crore (notification dated 24-03-2020). Application must include: record of default, proposed IRP name. NCLT has 14 days to ascertain default and admit or reject.

A

APPLICATION

In the present case, [FC] is owed a financial debt (money borrowed against payment of interest — §5(8)(a)) of ₹[X] which exceeds ₹1 crore. The corporate debtor has failed to pay the amount due on [date], constituting a "default" under §3(12). No disciplinary proceedings are pending against the proposed IRP. The application is complete in all respects. [Address any special condition if allottees/real estate scenario — joint filing threshold applies.]

C

CONCLUSION

The application by [FC] is maintainable under §7. NCLT should admit the application, declare moratorium, make public announcement, and appoint the proposed IRP on the insolvency commencement date.

IRAC 2 — Moratorium & Effect on Guarantor

IRAC · TEMPLATE · §14 MORATORIUM — GUARANTOR PROTECTION
I

ISSUE

Whether the creditor can initiate recovery proceedings against [personal/corporate guarantor] during the moratorium period declared against the corporate debtor.

R

RULE

§14(1) prohibits institution of suits and proceedings against the corporate debtor during moratorium. However, §14(3) expressly states that the moratorium restrictions do NOT apply to "a surety in a contract of guarantee to a corporate debtor."

A

APPLICATION

[Guarantor] is a surety in a contract of guarantee to the corporate debtor. The moratorium under §14 protects only the corporate debtor — it does not extend to guarantors. Approval of a resolution plan also does not ipso facto discharge a personal guarantor (SC in Lalit Kumar Jain). The creditor may independently pursue the guarantor for recovery.

C

CONCLUSION

The creditor can validly initiate recovery proceedings / bankruptcy proceedings against [guarantor] even while moratorium is in force against the corporate debtor. The moratorium does not provide any shield to the guarantor.

IRAC 3 — Resolution Plan Approval & Binding Effect

IRAC · TEMPLATE · §31 RESOLUTION PLAN — BINDING EFFECT
I

ISSUE

Whether [creditor / government authority] can claim dues from the corporate debtor that were not addressed in the approved resolution plan.

R

RULE

Under §31, an approved resolution plan is binding on the corporate debtor, its employees, members, creditors, guarantors, and all other stakeholders including Central Government, State Governments, and Local Authorities. SC in Essar Steel (2019): successful resolution applicant starts on a "fresh slate" — all liabilities beyond what is in the plan are extinguished.

A

APPLICATION

The resolution plan was approved by the CoC (≥66% voting share) and subsequently by the NCLT. [Creditor/Authority's] claim was either included in the plan at a specified amount or was extinguished. Post-approval, no additional claim can be made against the corporate debtor as the plan creates a complete discharge of pre-plan liabilities not included in the plan.

C

CONCLUSION

[Creditor / Authority] cannot recover dues not provided for in the approved resolution plan. The plan's binding effect under §31 extinguishes all such claims. The new management operates the CD on a fresh slate.

Essential Definitions — Quick Reference (§3 & §5)

Financial Debt §5(8)

Debt + interest disbursed against consideration for time value of money. Includes bonds, debentures, finance leases, real estate allottee amounts, derivatives (at market value)

Operational Debt §5(21)

Claim in respect of goods/services (incl. employment) or statutory dues payable to Central/State/Local Govt.

Default §3(12)

Non-payment of whole or any part / instalment of debt when due. Includes default to any FC, not just the applicant FC.

Corporate Person §3(7)

Company + LLP + any body with limited liability. Excludes financial service providers.

Dispute §5(6)

Suit/arbitration re: existence of debt, quality of goods/services, or breach of representation/warranty

Voting Share §5(28)

Single FC's share of voting rights in CoC = proportion of financial debt owed to FC / total financial debt of CD

Resolution Professional §5(27)

IP appointed to conduct CIRP / PPIRP; includes interim RP

Insolvency Commencement Date §5(12)

Date of admission of application by AA under §7, 9, or 10 — the clock starts here
